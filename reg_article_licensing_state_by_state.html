How Online Casino Licensing Works (State by State)

Complete US Regulatory Guide for 2024-2025

Online casino licensing in the United States is a complex state-by-state process with each jurisdiction maintaining its own requirements, costs, and approval timelines. This comprehensive guide breaks down the licensing process, requirements, and current status across all US states.

Overview of US Online Gambling Regulation

Federal vs State Authority

Online gambling in the US is primarily regulated at the state level following key federal legislation:

  • Wire Act (1961): Originally banned interstate sports betting, reinterpreted in 2011 to allow states to legalize online casino gaming
  • UIGEA (2006): Unlawful Internet Gambling Enforcement Act - doesn't prohibit gambling but regulates financial transactions
  • PASPA Repeal (2018): Supreme Court struck down federal sports betting ban, opening door for state-level legalization
  • State Sovereignty: Each state decides whether to legalize, how to regulate, and which games to permit
Key Principle: Online casino gaming is legal if explicitly permitted by state law. It's not automatically legal in states that haven't addressed itâ€”operators must obtain proper licensing in each state where they operate.

Regulatory Body: Delaware Lottery

Market Size: $300 million (2023)

Licensing Requirements:

  • State-Run Model: Delaware Lottery operates online casinos directly (no private operator licensing)
  • Casino Partnership: 3 retail casinos participate (Dover Downs, Harrington, Delaware Park)
  • Revenue Share: Casinos receive percentage of online revenue generated
  • Tax Treatment: Revenue flows to state as lottery proceeds

Unique Model:

Delaware's state-run monopoly model means there's no traditional licensing process for private operators. The lottery contracts with game suppliers and technology providers but maintains operational control.

General Licensing Process (Typical Timeline)

MONTH 1-2: Pre-Application

Review state requirements, identify casino partner (if required), preliminary financial review, legal structure setup.

MONTH 3-4: Application Submission

Complete application forms (50-200 pages), submit supporting documents, pay initial fees, provide financial statements, disclose all principals and key employees.

MONTH 5-8: Background Investigation

State conducts FBI fingerprint checks, financial investigations, reviews business plan, interviews principals, investigates business associates, reviews international operations (if any).

MONTH 9-10: Technical Review

Gaming platform reviewed by state-approved testing lab, RNG certification, game library certification, geolocation testing, responsible gaming tool verification.

MONTH 11: Public Hearing

Application presented to gaming board, public comment period (if required), board asks questions, addresses any concerns.

MONTH 12: License Approval

Board votes on application, conditional approval granted, final compliance checks, license issued, soft launch testing.

Common Application Requirements

1. Corporate Documentation

  • Articles of incorporation
  • Operating agreements / bylaws
  • Ownership structure charts
  • List of all shareholders >5%
  • Board member information

2. Financial Documents

  • 3 years audited financial statements
  • Current balance sheet and income statement
  • Tax returns (corporate and personal for principals)
  • Bank references and credit reports
  • Proof of minimum capitalization
  • Business plan with 5-year financial projections

3. Personal Background Information (All Principals)

  • Personal history questionnaire (20-40 pages per person)
  • Employment history (10+ years)
  • Residential history (10+ years)
  • Criminal background authorization
  • Credit authorization
  • Professional references
  • Educational credentials

4. Technical Documentation

  • Description of gaming platform
  • RNG certification from approved lab
  • Game library with RTP percentages
  • Server and data center information
  • Disaster recovery plan
  • Cybersecurity protocols
  • Player protection measures

5. Compliance Documentation

  • Anti-money laundering (AML) program
  • Responsible gaming policy and tools
  • Age verification procedures
  • Geolocation verification system
  • Self-exclusion program participation
  • Advertising and marketing compliance plan

Disqualifying Factors

States may deny licenses based on:

  • Criminal History: Felony convictions, gambling-related offenses, financial crimes
  • Financial Issues: Bankruptcy, tax liens, poor credit, insufficient capitalization
  • Previous Violations: Gaming violations in other jurisdictions, regulatory sanctions
  • Unsuitable Associates: Business relationships with disqualified individuals
  • Incomplete Application: Missing documents, false information, non-cooperation
  • Foreign Operations: Operating in black/grey markets (major concern)
Critical Issue - Previous Unregulated Markets: Many international operators face scrutiny for past operations in unregulated or prohibited markets. US regulators closely examine whether applicants operated in states where online gambling was illegal or in foreign grey markets. This has led to denial or delays for several major operators.

Multi-State Licensing Strategy

Expansion Sequence (Typical)

  1. First State: Apply in most favorable market (often New Jersey - fastest approval, most experience reviewing applications)
  2. Leverage First License: Use NJ approval to demonstrate credibility in other states
  3. Pennsylvania: High cost but large market - apply once NJ operational
  4. Michigan: Fast-growing market, reasonable fees
  5. Additional States: As new states legalize

Timing Considerations

  • Apply in multiple states simultaneously if financially feasible
  • Background investigation only done once - share results across states (in most cases)
  • Technical certifications often transferable between states
  • Total time to 3 states: 12-18 months with overlapping applications

Ongoing Compliance Requirements

Once licensed, operators must maintain continuous compliance:

  • Monthly Reporting: Revenue reports, tax payments, player statistics
  • Quarterly Financials: Updated financial statements submitted quarterly
  • Annual Audits: Independent financial audits, technical audits
  • Material Changes: Report ownership changes, new key employees, platform changes
  • Incident Reporting: Report system outages, security breaches, player disputes
  • Random Inspections: Regulators may inspect operations without notice
  • Annual Renewal: License renewal process with updated documents
