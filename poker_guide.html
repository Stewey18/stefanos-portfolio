Understanding Texas Hold'em

Texas Hold'em is played with a standard 52-card deck. Each player receives two private cards (hole cards), and five community cards are dealt face-up on the table. Players make the best possible five-card poker hand using any combination of their hole cards and the community cards.

Game Structure

2 Hole Cards: Private cards dealt to each player face-down

5 Community Cards: Shared cards dealt in three stages:

  • The Flop: First three community cards
  • The Turn: Fourth community card
  • The River: Fifth and final community card

How a Hand is Played

A Texas Hold'em hand progresses through several betting rounds. Here's the complete flow:

1. Blinds Posted

The two players to the left of the dealer button post the small blind and big blind (forced bets to start the action).

2. Hole Cards Dealt

Each player receives two private cards face-down. These are your hole cards that only you can see.

3. Pre-Flop Betting

First betting round begins with the player to the left of the big blind. Players can fold, call, or raise.

4. The Flop

Three community cards are dealt face-up in the center of the table. Second betting round begins.

5. The Turn

A fourth community card is dealt. Third betting round begins.

6. The River

The fifth and final community card is dealt. Final betting round begins.

7. Showdown

If multiple players remain, they reveal their hands. The best five-card poker hand wins the pot!

Player Actions

During each betting round, you have several options:

FOLD

Give up your hand and forfeit any chance of winning the pot. Use when your hand is weak.

CHECK

Pass the action without betting. Only available if no one has bet yet in this round.

CALL

Match the current bet to stay in the hand. Required to continue if someone has bet.

BET

Put chips into the pot. The first person to add chips in a betting round "bets."

RAISE

Increase the current bet amount. Forces other players to call your raise or fold.

ALL-IN

Bet all your remaining chips. You can't be forced out but can't win more than what you've matched.

Common Beginner Mistakes

Playing Too Many Hands: The number one mistake. Be selective‚Äîfold most hands pre-flop.

Ignoring Position: Playing the same hands from every position is costly. Tighten up in early position.

Calling Too Much: If you're not confident enough to raise, you should probably fold. Avoid being a "calling station."

Playing on Tilt: Making emotional decisions after a bad beat. Take a break if you're frustrated.

Neglecting Bankroll Management: Playing at stakes too high for your bankroll leads to going broke.
